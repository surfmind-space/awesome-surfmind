# Audience playbook

Read this when mapping rounds to audiences, drafting the question bank, or coaching what to lead with. Each audience has a different focus, a different set of likely questions, and a different disclosure strategy. When the schedule is unknown, treat these as the likely audiences and present them separately.

## Recruiter screen

- **Focus:** compensation, logistics, timeline, location, and motivation. This round filters, it rarely sells.
- **Likely questions:** "Why this company?", "What are you looking for in your next role?", "What's your timeline?", "What are your compensation expectations?", "Are you authorized to work / do you need sponsorship?"
- **Lead with:** motivation and fit. Open with why this company before anything else.
- **Compensation, when leverage is thin:** defer a hard number rather than anchoring yourself. Use: "I'm calibrating to market for this level; can you share the band?" If they press, give a wide researched range, not a point.

## Hiring manager

- **Focus:** scope of the role, the team's current challenge, and the first 90 days.
- **Likely questions:** "Walk me through a project you owned end to end.", "How would you approach your first 90 days here?", "Tell me about a time you handled [challenge the team faces].", "Where do you want to grow?"
- **Lead with:** a link from the user's background to a named team challenge. Tie a real story to the specific mandate in the job description or what the manager has said.

## Peer or technical panel

- **Focus:** stack, systems, coding, design, or other role specific depth.
- **Likely questions:** stack and systems design probes, a coding or case exercise, "How would you debug / scale / structure X?", tradeoff discussions.
- **Lead with:** stack relevant proof. Open with the most relevant hands on work, then reason out loud through the exercise.

## Mixed panel

- **Focus:** several of the above in one session, often back to back.
- **Strategy:** vary a story's angle to fit each interviewer (impact for the manager, mechanics for the engineer) but never contradict yourself on compensation, timeline, motivation, or the facts of a story across the panel. Panels compare notes.

## Questions to ask back

Always reference the role, team, product, or company — generic questions read as low effort. Tailor by audience:

- **Recruiter:** "What does the interview process look like end to end?", "What's the team's hiring timeline?"
- **Hiring manager:** "What does the first 0→1 win look like for this role over the next two quarters?", "What's the biggest challenge facing the team right now?"
- **Peer panel:** "What does the on call / review / release process feel like day to day?", "What's the most painful part of the current system?"

## Disclosure guardrails

Coach honesty under pressure, never deception. Do **not** tell the user to lie or misrepresent on:

- Compensation, current or expected.
- Competing offers — if there is no real one, deferring is the move, not inventing one.
- Work authorization or sponsorship needs.
- Notice period or availability.

Deferring, framing, and leading with strengths are fair. Fabricating a number, an offer, or a fact is not.
